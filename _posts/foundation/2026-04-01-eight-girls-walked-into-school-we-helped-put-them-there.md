I want to tell you how this started. Not with a strategy or a grant application or a carefully worded mission statement. It started with a child.

I was visiting family in central India, a state of dense forests, red earth, and a quiet, particular beauty that gets under your skin. Our housekeeper had brought her daughter to work that morning. She was six years old. She sat on the kitchen floor and watched the adults move around her, and I watched her, and something in me simply refused to accept that this was normal.

It is normal, of course. Across India, and across much of the world, this is what poverty looks like not dramatic, not visible from a distance, just a six-year-old on a kitchen floor on a Tuesday morning when she should have been sitting at a desk, learning her letters, being somewhere that was hers. The family needed the income. The mother needed to work. And so the child came along.

I said something. Nothing changed.

But I kept thinking about her, about the particular quiet of a child who has already learned not to ask for too much or anything. And I started asking different questions. Not what can I do in this moment, but what structures exist that actually work. What organisations have figured out, patiently and over years, how to get girls into school and keep them there.

That is how we found Educate Girls.

They have been working in rural India for years, building something that actually lasts — community volunteers, family conversations, enrolment drives, follow-through. Not charity in the transactional sense, but the slow, unglamorous work of changing what a village believes is possible for its daughters.

Through Everwell, we supported the enrolment of eight girls in Chhattisgarh this year. Eight. I know that sounds small. I have spent some time sitting with that number, turning it over. Eight girls who will learn to read. Eight girls whose mothers will watch them go to school in the morning. Eight futures that have quietly, without fanfare, shifted.

We don't know their names. We never will — and that feels right. This was never about us. It was about a child on a kitchen floor, and the stubborn, hopeful belief that her story didn't have to end there.
