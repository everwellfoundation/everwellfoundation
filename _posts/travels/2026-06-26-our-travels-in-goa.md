There is a particular kind of peace that belongs only to places caught between seasons. We arrived in Whistler in that narrow window — the hikers had gone home, the skiers hadn't arrived yet, and the village belonged, almost entirely, to us.

The drive up from Vancouver sets the tone. The Sea-to-Sky Highway is one of those roads that demands you put your phone away. Ocean on one side, mountain on the other, the whole thing feeling slightly unreal — like someone had decided that one view wasn't enough and simply stacked two of them together.

Whistler village in the shoulder season is a different place to the one you see in the brochures. The gondolas sit still. The restaurants have tables free. You can walk the Village Stroll — that long, pedestrianised heart of the town — and actually hear your own footsteps on the pavement, the crunch of the first fallen leaves, the distant sound of water somewhere you can't quite place.

We spent most of our time doing very little, which is harder than it sounds. Coffee in the morning, slow. A walk that had no particular destination. Lunch somewhere with a mountain view, lingered over. There is an art to being somewhere without trying to see everything, and Whistler in the off-season teaches it well.

The colours were extraordinary. The aspens had turned — gold and amber and a deep, burning red — set against the granite grey of the mountains behind them and the first dustings of snow on the peaks above. It looked, genuinely, like a painting. The kind you'd dismiss as too perfect if someone had made it up.

One afternoon we found ourselves at Alta Lake — still, glassy, the mountains reflected perfectly in the water. We sat there for a while without saying much. Some places make conversation feel unnecessary.

What we took home from Whistler wasn't a list of things we'd done. It was the particular feeling of a place at rest — unhurried, unperformed, just itself. That is rarer than it sounds, and worth travelling for.
